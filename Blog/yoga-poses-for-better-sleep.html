5 Yoga Poses for Better Sleep: A Science-Backed Bedtime Routine

Struggling to sleep? Discover 5 science-backed yoga poses for better sleep and a simple 20-minute bedtime routine to help you fall asleep faster.

A dedicated bedtime yoga routine can signal your body it's time for deep rest

We are living through a global sleep crisis. Between the persistent "always-on" culture of remote work and the blue light emitted by our late-night screen time, the quality of our rest has plummeted. For the busy professional—whether you are finishing a shift in London or starting your evening in New York—the inability to switch off the brain is a significant barrier to health. Stress doesn't just stay in the mind; it manifests as physical tension that makes falling asleep feel like an impossible task. However, research and tradition suggest a natural solution: just 20 minutes of a dedicated bedtime yoga routine can signal to your body that it is time to transition from high-alert to deep rest. By incorporating specific yoga for better sleep, you can bypass the frustration of tossing and turning and dive into restorative slumber.

Takeaway: A short, intentional yoga practice before bed is the most effective natural antidote to modern sleep-disrupting stressors.

Why Yoga Works for Sleep — The Science

Yoga is not just stretching; it is a sophisticated method of regulating the nervous system. According to the sources, yoga physically changes your body's stress response and promotes relaxation at a cellular level. When you practice yoga for better sleep, you are consciously moving your body from the sympathetic nervous system (fight or flight) into the parasympathetic nervous system (rest and digest).

At a physiological level, this shift helps lower cortisol—the hormone responsible for alertness—while creating the ideal environment for melatonin, the sleep hormone, to rise. By focusing on breath-centered movement, you lower your heart rate and blood pressure, preparing every organ in your body for the recovery process that only deep sleep provides.

Takeaway: Yoga facilitates a cellular shift from high-stress alertness to a deep, parasympathetic state of relaxation.

1. Legs Up The Wall (Viparita Karani)

How to do it: Sit sideways as close to a wall as possible, then gently swing your legs up onto the wall as you lie back on the floor. Keep your arms relaxed at your sides, palms facing up, and your neck in a neutral position.

Why it specifically helps sleep: This pose is a cornerstone of virtual yoga classes for relaxation because it reverses the blood flow, helping to drain fluid from tired legs and feet. It acts as a powerful "reset" for the nervous system, immediately lowering the heart rate and quieting a racing mind.

How long to hold: 5 to 10 minutes.

Takeaway: Viparita Karani is the ultimate restorative inversion to quiet the mind and physically decompress after a long day.

2. Child's Pose (Balasana)

How to do it: Kneel on the floor, touch your big toes together, and sit on your heels. Fold forward, resting your forehead on the mat and stretching your arms in front of you or alongside your body.

Why it specifically helps sleep: This pose is frequently used in Zuga’s online yoga classes to encourage introspection and calm. By folding inward, you create a "sensory withdrawal" that helps shut out the distractions of the day, while the gentle stretch in the lower back releases physical patterns of stress.

How long to hold: 2 to 3 minutes, focusing on deep belly breaths.

Takeaway: Balasana provides a safe, grounded space to withdraw from the world's noise and settle into your internal rhythm.

3. Seated Forward Bend (Paschimottanasana)

How to do it: Sit with your legs stretched out in front of you. Inhale to lengthen your spine, and exhale as you fold forward from your hips, reaching for your shins or feet.

Why it specifically helps sleep: Forward bends are essential yoga poses for sleep because they are naturally cooling and calming. According to Zuga’s experts, these techniques are vital for "energy and calm". Stretching the entire back of the body helps release the tension held in the spine and hamstrings, which is often a result of sitting at a desk all day.

How long to hold: 1 to 2 minutes.

Takeaway: This pose helps release spinal tension and "cools" the nervous system, preparing the brain for a night of deep rest.

4. Supine Spinal Twist (Supta Matsyendrasana)

How to do it: Lie on your back and hug your knees to your chest. Drop both knees to one side while keeping your shoulders grounded and your gaze toward the opposite hand.

Why it specifically helps sleep: Twists are known for their "detoxifying" effect on the digestive system, which can often be sluggish due to stress. Releasing the muscles around the spine helps to "unwind" the physical knots of the day, making it one of the most effective yoga poses for sleep to do actually in your bed.

How long to hold: 1 minute on each side.

Takeaway: Spinal twists physically "unwind" the day's stress from your back, allowing for a more comfortable and uninterrupted sleep.

5. Corpse Pose with Pranayama (Savasana)

How to do it: Lie flat on your back with your arms and legs spread slightly. Close your eyes and practice "Abdominal Breathing" or "Box Breathing" as taught in Zuga’s Online Pranayama Classes.

Why it specifically helps sleep: This is the most critical part of a night yoga routine. By focusing on the breath, you improve lung capacity and reduce anxiety. According to Zuga’s mission of "Yoga Without Borders," these guided techniques for clarity and calm are the perfect sleep fix for practitioners worldwide. Savasana allows the body to fully integrate the relaxation response, ensuring you don't just "go to sleep," but stay in a high-quality sleep state.

How long to hold: 5 to 10 minutes (or until you drift off).

Takeaway: Combining total physical stillness with expert breathwork is the gold standard for treating insomnia and improving sleep quality.
